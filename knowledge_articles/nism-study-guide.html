UNIT 1: Concept and Role of a Mutual Fund

Weightage: 6% (~6 Questions)

Definition and Basic Concept

A mutual fund is a pool of money collected from many investors with similar investment objectives, managed by a professional fund manager on their behalf. The pooled money is invested in securities/assets according to the fund's stated investment objective.

Key Characteristics

  • Collective Investment: Money pooled from many investors
  • Professional Management: Managed by qualified fund managers
  • Diversification: Spreads investment across multiple securities
  • Regulated Structure: Operates under SEBI regulations
  • Liquidity: Investors can buy/sell units easily

Advantages vs. Disadvantages

Advantage Explanation
DiversificationReduces unsystematic risk by spreading across securities
Professional ManagementExpert fund managers make investment decisions
AffordabilityLow minimum investment (₹500-5,000)
LiquidityEasy redemption without transaction delays
TransparencyRegular NAV publication and reports
Tax EfficiencyCertain schemes offer tax benefits (ELSS)
RegulationProtected by SEBI oversight
Disadvantages/Risks: Expense Ratio (fees reduce returns), Liquidity Risk (in stress), and Market Risk (value fluctuation).

UNIT 2: Fund Structure and Constituents

Weightage: 4% (~4 Questions)

The "Four-Pillar" Structure

Indian Mutual Funds typically follow a 3-tier structure (Sponsor -> Trustee -> AMC), but operationally involve four key constituents.

Entity Role & Key Functions
Sponsor The promoter who starts the fund. Must contribute at least 40% of the AMC's net worth.
Trustee Protects Investor Interests. Holds the property of the mutual fund in trust for the benefit of unit holders. Supervises the AMC.
AMC (Asset Management Co.) Investment Manager. Handles day-to-day operations, investment decisions, and marketing.
Custodian Safeguards Assets. Holds the securities (shares/bonds) physically or electronically. Independent of the AMC.

Other Service Providers

  • RTA (Registrar & Transfer Agent): Maintains investor records (KYC, Units held, Buying/Selling requests). Examples: CAMS, KFintech.
  • Auditors: Internal and Statutory auditors to ensure accounts are accurate.
  • Distributors: Sell units to investors (Banks, IFAs, Brokers).

UNIT 3: Legal and Regulatory Environment

High Priority Unit: 10% Weightage (~10 Questions)

SEBI Regulatory Framework

The Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) is the primary regulator.

  • SEBI (Mutual Fund) Regulations, 1996: The primary regulation governing MFs.
  • Objective: Protecting Investors, Developing Markets.

KYC (Know Your Customer)

Mandatory for all investors before investing.

  • Documents: Identity Proof (PAN/Aadhaar) + Address Proof.
  • In-Person Verification (IPV): Mandatory.
  • CKYC: Central KYC registry ensures you only do KYC once across the financial sector.

SEBI Categorization of Schemes

UNIT 4: Offer Documents

Weightage: 6% (~6 Questions)

Key Documents

  1. SID (Scheme Information Document): Detailed legal document. Contains investment objective, asset allocation, fees, risk factors.
  2. SAI (Statement of Additional Information): Contains statutory information about the Mutual Fund house (Sponsors, Trustees). Standard across all schemes of that fund.
  3. KIM (Key Information Memorandum): A summary of the SID. Must accompany every application form. Contains essential info like loads, risks, and historical performance.

UNIT 5: Fund Distribution

Weightage: 8% (~8 Questions)

Distribution Channels

  • DISTRIBUTION CHANNELS
    • Direct Channels (No Commission)
      • AMC Website / Mobile App
      • AMC Branch Office
    • Indirect Channels (Commission based)
      • Individual Financial Advisors (IFAs)
      • National Distributors (NJ India, Prudent)
      • Banks (Bancassurance)
      • Stock Brokers
    • Online Platforms
      • MF Utilities, BSE Star MF, NSE NMF II

Regular vs. Direct Plans

  • Regular Plan: Sold through a distributor. Expense ratio is higher (includes distributor commission).
  • Direct Plan: Sold directly by AMC. Expense ratio is lower. Return is slightly higher.
Commission Disclosure: Distributors must disclose the commissions they earn to the investor. They cannot charge entry loads.

UNIT 6: Accounting, Valuation & Taxation

High Priority Unit: 10% Weightage (~10 Questions)

Net Asset Value (NAV)

NAV = (Total Assets - Total Liabilities) / Number of Units Outstanding
  • Calculated Daily (at market close).
  • Published by 11 AM next day (usually same day evening).
  • Cut-off Time:
    • Liquid Funds: 1:30 PM (Historical NAV applies)
    • Other Funds: 3:00 PM (Closing NAV applies)

UNIT 7: Investor Services

Weightage: 12% (Highest Weightage)

Investment Transactions

  • Lump Sum: One-time investment.
  • SIP (Systematic Investment Plan): Fixed amount at regular intervals. Good for rupee cost averaging.
  • STP (Systematic Transfer Plan): Transfer from one scheme (usually liquid) to another (usually equity) regularly.
  • SWP (Systematic Withdrawal Plan): Withdraw fixed amount regularly. Good for retirement income.

Redemption Process

  • T+2 or T+3 settlement for Equity/Debt funds.
  • Exit Loads may apply if redeemed early (e.g., 1% if redeemed within 1 year).

UNIT 8: Return, Risk and Performance

Weightage: 10% (~10 Questions)

Types of Returns

  • Absolute Return: Simple gain/loss. Good for < 1 year.
  • CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate): The industry standard for periods > 1 year.
  • XIRR: Used for SIPs or multiple cash flows.

Risk Metrics

Metric What it measures Interpretation
Standard DeviationTotal VolatilityHigher = Riskier
BetaVolatility relative to MarketBeta > 1 means more volatile than market
Sharpe RatioRisk-adjusted ReturnHigher is better (more return for same risk)
AlphaExcess return over benchmarkPositive alpha = Manager beat the market

UNIT 9: Scheme Selection

Weightage: 10% (~10 Questions)

Equity Categories

  • Large Cap: Top 100 companies. Stable.
  • Mid Cap: 101st to 250th companies. Higher growth, higher risk.
  • Small Cap: 251st onwards. Very high risk.
  • Multi Cap: Minimum 25% in each (Large, Mid, Small).
  • ELSS: Tax saving, 3-year lock-in.

Debt Categories

  • Liquid Fund: Up to 91 days maturity. For parking cash.
  • Overnight Fund: 1 day maturity. Safest.
  • Gilt Fund: Govt securities. No credit risk, but has interest rate risk.

UNIT 11: Financial Planning

Life Cycle Stages

  1. Accumulation Phase (Age 20-45): High risk taking ability. Focus on Equity for wealth creation.
  2. Transition Phase (Age 45-60): Approaching goals. Shift towards balance (Equity + Debt).
  3. Distribution Phase (Retirement): Low risk ability. Focus on Debt/SWP for regular income.
Financial Goal Setting: Goals must be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-bound).

UNIT 12: Model Portfolios

Standard Allocation Rules

Rule of 100: Equity Allocation = (100 - Age). Example: Age 30 should have 70% Equity.

  • Aggressive Portfolio (Long Term, >10 years)
    • 80% Equity (Mid/Small Cap focus)
    • 20% Debt
  • Moderate Portfolio (Medium Term, 5-10 years)
    • 60% Equity (Large Cap/Flexi Cap)
    • 40% Debt
  • Conservative Portfolio (Short Term, <3 years)
    • 10-20% Equity
    • 80-90% Debt (Short duration/Liquid)

Key Formulas Reference

1. NAV = (Assets - Liabilities) / Units

2. Real Return = (1 + Nominal Return) / (1 + Inflation Rate) - 1

3. Cost of Acquisition (Indexed) = Original Cost * (CII Year of Sale / CII Year of Purchase)

4. Sharpe Ratio = (Scheme Return - Risk Free Rate) / Standard Deviation

5. Treynor Ratio = (Scheme Return - Risk Free Rate) / Beta
